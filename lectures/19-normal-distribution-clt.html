Sampling distribution of the mean

All of the tools that we've learned for generating the sampling distribution apply to the mean.

However, the mean has some special properties that give us a short cut for generating the sampling distribution.

Look for the pattern in the sampling distribution of the mean for 4 very different distributions.

Distribution 1 or 4: Normal

Distribution 2 or 4: Exponential

Distribution 3 or 4: Mixture of Normals

Distribution 3 or 4: Mixture of Betas

Sampling distribution of the mean

Pattern: The sampling distribution of the mean seems to be symmetric and bell shaped, (almost) regardless of the distribution.

Sampling distribution of the mean

Pattern: The sampling distribution of the mean seems to be symmetric and bell shaped, (almost) regardless of the distribution.

Precise statement: If \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(\dots\) are independent random variables, all having the same distribution which has mean \(m\) and variance \(v^2\), then \[ Z = \frac{\bar{X}_N - m}{\frac{v}{\sqrt{N}}} = \sqrt{N}\frac{\bar{X}_N - m}{v} \] converges in distribution to a standard normal random variable.

Central Limit Theorem

Key phrases

If \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(\dots\) are independent random variables, all having the same distribution which has mean \(m\) and variance \(v^2\), \[ Z = \frac{\bar{X}_N - m}{\frac{v}{\sqrt{N}}} = \sqrt{N}\frac{\bar{X}_N - m}{v} \] converges in distribution to a standard normal random variable.

  1. Independent
  2. Identically distributed
  3. Mean and variance exist
  4. Sample size large enough for convergence

Where does the CLT come from?

If \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(\dots\) are independent random Normal variables with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), then

\[ \sum_{i=1}^N X_i \sim N(N\mu, N\sigma^2)\] and

\[\bar{X}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N X_i \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{N}\right) \]

Where does the CLT come from? (continued)

After standardization,

\[ \frac{\bar{X}_N - \mu}{\sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}{N}}} \sim N\left(0, 1\right) \]

which simplifies to

\[ \sqrt{N}\frac{\bar{X}_N - \mu}{\sigma} \sim N\left(0, 1\right) \]

Where does the CLT come from? (continued)

If \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(\dots\) are independent random Normal variables with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), then \[ \sqrt{N}\frac{\bar{X}_N - \mu}{\sigma} \sim N\left(0, 1\right) \] regardless of the size of N.

Where does the CLT come from? (continued)

It can be shown that \[ \sqrt{N}\frac{\bar{X}_N - \mu}{\sigma}\] has an approximate standard normal distribution regardless of the distribution so long as
1. the distribution mean and variance exist
2. the sample size is large enough
3. the data \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(\ldots\), \(X_N\) are independent and identically distributed.

Short-cut for the sampling distribution of the mean

\[\text{If } Z \text{ is a standard normal draw, then } \\ Z\frac{v}{\sqrt{N}} + m \\ \text{ is an approximate draw from the sampling distribution of }\\ \bar{X}_N \\ \text{when the conditions of the CLT are met.}\]

Short-cut for the sampling distribution of the mean

\[\text{If } Z_{\alpha} \text{ is a quantile of the standard normal, then} \\ Z_{\alpha}\frac{v}{\sqrt{N}} + m \\ \text{ is an approximate quantile of the sampling distribution of }\\ \bar{X}_N\\ \text{when the conditions of the CLT are met.}\]

Short-cut for the sampling distribution of the mean

\[\text{Consequently, }\\ \left(Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{v}{\sqrt{N}} + m, Z_{1-\alpha/2}\frac{v}{\sqrt{N}} + m \right) \\ \text{ is an approximate confidence interval of }\\ \bar{X}_N\\ \text{when the conditions of the CLT are met.}\]

Question: What if one doesn't know what \(m\) and \(v\) are?

One approach is to plug-in the sample estimates, \(\bar{X}\) for \(m\) and \(s\) for \(v\):

\[ \left(Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}} + \bar{X}_N,\ Z_{1-\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}} + \bar{X}_N \right) \\ \text{ is an approximate confidence interval of }\\ \bar{X}_N\]

Short-cut for the sampling distribution of the mean

Question:What if one doesn't know what \(m\) and \(v\) are?

One approach is to plug-in the sample estimates, \(\bar{X}\) for \(m\) and \(s\) for \(v\). Often, when the plug-in solution is implemented, the standard normal quantiles are replaced with \(t\) quantiles:

\[ \left(t_{\alpha/2, N-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}} + \bar{X}_N,\ t_{1-\alpha/2, N-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}} + \bar{X}_N \right) \\ \text{ is an approximate confidence interval of }\\ \bar{X}_N\]

Short-cut for the sampling distribution of the mean

Lingo: \(Z\) intervals use quantiles from the standard normal. \(t\) intervals use quantiles from the \(t_{N-1}\) distribution.

Why \(t\) intervals? To capture additional uncertainty related to estimating the standard deviation.

Why does the author of our text not care about \(t\) intervals? For large \(N\) the difference between \(Z\) and \(t_{N-1}\) is very small.

More notation & lingo

Because the standard normal and \(t\) distributions are symmetric

\[Z_{1-\alpha/2} = - Z_{\alpha/2}\ \text{ and } \\ t_{1-\alpha/2,\ df} = - t_{\alpha/2,\ df},\]

The simplified expression of the confidence interval is

\[ \bar{X} \pm Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}\\ \bar{X} \pm t_{\alpha/2, N-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}\]

Any summary measure that can be framed as a sample average can use the CLT short-cut

\[ \text{If } X_i = \left\{\begin{array}{ll} 1 & Smoker \\ 0 & Nonsmoker \end{array} \right.\\ \text{Then }\hat{p} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i = 1}^N X_i = \bar{X}_N \text{ is the sample proportion}\]

Proportion and the CLT short-cut

What is the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}\)?

CLT trick

\[X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p) \\ E[X_i] = p\\ V[X_i] = p(1-p)\\ \ \\ \text{So, } \hat{p} \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} N\left(p, \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{N}}\right) \\ \text{Plug-in, } \hat{p} \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} N\left(\hat{p}, \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{N}}\right) \\ \]

Confidence interval for proportion using CLT short cut

\[ \hat{p} \pm Z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{N}} \]

Lingo

Confidence intervals calculated with the CLT short cut are often call Wald intervals.

  • For small samples, Wald CIs for the proportion tend to be too narrow
  • Another problem with Wald CIs: can extend below 0 & above 1.

Aside

Though not as widely known, Wilson or score CIs have better coverage than Wald, especially for smaller samples and proportions near 0 or 1.
